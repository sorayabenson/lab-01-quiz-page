Let's Print!

Printmaking is so rad! It is a craft that has so many different possibilities and techniques. Each technique has it's own texture and language.

There are so many different techniques:

  • Intaglio
  • Relief
  • Risograph
  • Screenprinting
  • Letterpress

But let me tell you about my favorite one to work with, relief. Relief printing uses an image carved into a block that you then apply ink to, and pull it off with paper.

Carve

With relief printing you can use any design you can imagine, from the most minimal to the most intricate. You can use wood blocks or linoleum to carve the design into. Wood has a great natural texture, while linoleum holds fine details beautifully.

To transfer the design on to the block you can use transfer paper between the design and the block and trace over the lines lightly. Another method is to print the image on a laser printer, tape the fresh print to the block face down and lightly rub the back of the paper with acetone or alcohol to transfer the image. Once the design is on the block you can use a sharpie to mark your lines more clearly to guide you while you carve.

Once the design is on the block it is time to carve! To carve you can use linoleum and woodcarving tools. This part is so relaxing. When you carve you are removing the negative space of the design, you are carving out the 'white' of the image. This part is the heartbeat of the piece. Play with how you make your marks, the way the lines interact with each other. Take your time. And most importantly keep your free hand behind your carving tool, dont accidently slice your fingers!

Ink

Once the block is carved it is time to ink it up! Use a piece of glass to mix your ink on with a paint spatula. Find the colors you love and keep mixing. You want the ink to be warm and pliable.

To roll the ink out, make a line of ink with the spatula the width of the brayer. Take the brayer and charge it up with ink and roll it out in a nice square. Build up the ink square by adding more ink until you have a nice orange peel texture.

And now it's time to ink your block! Roll the brayer over the block starting at one side and making your way across to the next. Recharge the brayer and roll the ink starting from a different corner. Build up the ink on the block layer by layer until it has the same texture as the ink pad.

Print

Let's pause and talk paper. Paper options are limitless. Lighter weight papers like Masa and Mulberry are great for pulling a print by hand, while heavier papers like Rives BFK or Hahnem√ºhle are really nice to work with on a printing press.

To finally print your image gently roll the paper onto the inked block and smooth it lightly with your hand, being very careful to not shift it on the block. Cover the paper with another piece of paper to protect the final print.

I'm going to explain how to handpull the print, because it is so accessible. To apply pressure and transfer the ink to the paper you can use a barren or a simple wooden spoon. Press and move in firm but gentle circles over the block. It takes quite a few passovers to fully print the image. You can peak to see how much of the ink has printed by rolling up a corner.

Once you get the feeling that you've pulled all the ink from the block, carefully roll the paper off the block and admire your sweet baby. Hang to dry, it takes a couple days.

Review Time!

These are the steps to make your own rad image:

  1. Make your design
  2. Transfer the image to the block
  3. Carve, carve, carve
  4. Mix your ink
  5. Roll the ink onto the block
  6. Carefully place the paper on the block
  7. Apply pressure to pull the image
  8. Let dry
